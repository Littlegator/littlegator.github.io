South Dakota School of Mines & Technology

My time was spent at SDSM&T being engaged as a student leader. As my GPA shows, my studies always came first, but that didn't get in the way of actively participating in the student community, organizations, and government. In student government, I was elected as hall council president, student senator, and held the finance chair position where I oversaw a $1.1MM budget. I decided to join a small budding fraternity on campus, where I was one of the founding members and recruitment chair. As recruitment chair, I grew the fraternity from 17 to 44 members in one year. I also volunteered for other positions on campus, such as a member of the Recreation Center Committee tasked with working with architects and contractors to orchestrate a $6.7MM remodel.


My education was driven by an experience-first model with an abundance of labs and experiments.The computer science minor focused on teaching C++, first, and the fundamental concepts of programming such as types, scoping, and object-oriented programming that it comprises. It then moved onto more complex topics like analysis of algorithms, artificial intelligence, and software engineering management. Along the way, we worked with other languages including C#, Java, LISP, PHP, and MySQL.


In the electrical engineering department, we started with basic concepts like digital logic and the fundamentals of circuits. We moved on to more complex things like electromagnetics, electronics, and semiconductor physics. I opted to take microwave engineering as an (incredibly interesting but equally stressful) elective, along with other courses such as real-time operating systems to compliment my computer engineering education.


The computer engineering curriculum focused on embedded systems. It included courses covering Embedded C and assembly language, mechatronics, control systems, digital signals and digital-signal processing, FPGA programming, and VLSI design. The curriculum relied heavily upon projects running on actual microcontroller boards, operating real devices we built such as autonomous robots, a temperature-sensing capacitive-touch-controlled mug, and a hydrogen fuel cell.

2012 - Now

Summer 2014

Electrical and Computer Engineering Intern

Raven Industries, Sioux Falls SD

Precision Agriculture: Autonomous and Smart Farm Equipment

During Summer 2015, I designed a system of ZigBees in a mesh network so devices could communicate with each other wirelessly during operation on farm equipment. These circuits were actually moving inside large equipment, meaning the vibration and static conditions were extremely rigorous.


With other interns, we designed a custom PCB with an Atmel ATmega328p (the same microprocessor as an Arduino Uno) that carried these ZigBees along with the rest of the payload. I was tasked with programming the ZigBees and writing the software for the microprocessors to exchange information among the devices. I also programmed a node on another custom PCB, using an Atmel ATmega1280, to intercept and process all the data, then send it off to a computer for operation.


This task included extensive work with AVR Embedded C, Atmel Studio, and JTAGICE3 in-system programming and debugging. We worked on teams with regular meetings, cooperation, and great friendships. All of this took place in Raven's rapid prototyping and rapid development team.
